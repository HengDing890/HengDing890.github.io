An Brief Summary of Question Classification

Posted on 2016-12-08

References:

[1] Li, Xin, and Dan Roth. “Learning question classifiers.” Proceedings of the 19th international conference on Computational linguistics-Volume 1. Association for Computational Linguistics, 2002. (Cited by 608)
[2] Hermjakob, Ulf. “Parsing and question classification for question answering.” Proceedings of the workshop on Open-domain question answering-Volume 12. Association for Computational Linguistics, 2001. (Cited by 180)
[3] Metzler, Donald, and W. Bruce Croft. “Analysis of statistical question classification for fact-based questions.” Information Retrieval 8.3 (2005): 481-504. (Cited by 98)

Notes of [1]

I think the paper is the most classical research about question classification. And it maybe is the first paper using machine learning approach to question classification.

1. Contributions

  • proposed a machine learning approach to question classification.
  • A layered semantic hierarchy of answer types

motivation: to categorize questions into different semantic classes that impose constraints on potential answers, so that they can be utilized in later stages of the question answering process.

2. question ontology

  • they define a two-layered taxonomy
    • first layer: 6 coarse classes
      • ABBREVIATION, ENTITY, DESCRIPTION, HUMAN, LOCATION and NUMERICVALUE
    • second layer: 50 fine classes

Notes of [2]

1. Contributions

  • Proposed an additional question treebank for question parsing
  • show the how quesiton parsing improve QA

motivation: penn treebank trained on newspaper corpus is not suitable for "question sentence

2. question ontology

  • Based on the follow paper, there are 122 categories (termed Qtargets in the paper) in their question ontology (question and answer types).
    • Abstract Qtarget
      • why-famous (What is Switzerland known for?)
      • abbreviation （How do you abbreviate limited partnership?）
      • definition (What is NAFTA)
      • contrast (What’s the difference between DARPA and NSF?)
      • ……
    • Quantity
      • energy-quantity
      • spatial-quantity
      • temporal-quantity
      • speed-quantity
      • numerical-quantity
      • ……
    • Qtargets referring to semantic role (Why can’t ostriches fly?)
    • Qtartgets referring to marked-up constituents (Name a film in which Jude Law acted)

queries: all their queries are from TREC 8&9 (QA task)

Notes of [3]

1. question ontology

  • Trec QA questions: 31 answer types, BBN’s question ontology
  • UIUC (a superset of the TREC QA track questions and impose a different question ontology on the data.): a hierarchical question ontology. 6 coarse grained classes and 50 fine grained classes.
  • MadSci (consists of science related fact-based questions): an augmented version of the TREC QA ontology.

2. evaluation metrices:

  • precision
  • P≤n
  • mean reciprocal rank.

3. baseline: bag of words features

  • Trec QA questions, 77.59% (precision) and 0.8437 (MRR)
  • UIUC, 86.20% (Coarse precision), 81.00% (fine precision), 0.9224 (Coarse MRR), 0.862 (fine MRR)
  • MadSci, 72.60% (precision) and 0.8124 (MRR)

4. features

  • Syntactic features: (questions of the same type often have the same syntactic style)
    • unigram and bigram features
    • POS tags
    • question word (what, who, where …), not use in the paper
    • presence of a proper noun phrase
  • Semantic features:
    • using wordnet to extract semantic information (WordNet hypernyms) about the headword of the main noun phrase
